## Current contract

- The building editor UI and editor-plugin activation must stay out of the runtime app shell. Reusable scene-node geometry types may cross the boundary only through an explicit narrow consumer contract; LowPolyTerrain3D optionally loads Street3D plus its transient intersection resolver for mask-derived assemblies without loading the dock, toolbar, or editor controllers.
- `Building3D` is the scene-owned root for one building assembly. A scene may contain multiple independent roots or packed building scene instances; selecting a root or any authored descendant makes it the active target, and the dock's Add Building command always creates and selects a new uniquely named root. Each root owns only its direct building-part lookup, geometry-change detection, and transient geometry resolvers, so merging and clipping never cross into a sibling building. No building-editor configuration is exported on the root; tool grid values, drawing constraints, and new-element defaults remain editor-dock state.
- `BuildingFactory` is the stateless construction boundary for wall, room, floor, street, stairs, standard rail, pillar, roof, and typed door/window nodes. `create_street_node()` receives a typed multi-point path plus explicit cross-section/stair settings and returns one detached configured Street3D; sampling can be requested explicitly by the Street tool or orchestrated by compatible terrain generation.
- The factory continues to own the concrete stair-layout, pillar, roof, and opening style registries, style-property application, unique child naming, shared placement helpers, and segment-aware opening transforms. `create_rail_node()` accepts total newel count, exact infill count per newel span, and newel thickness. `create_stairs_node()` accepts the stair-body, layout, rail, newel, infill, tread-style, and nosing settings dictionary and instantiates the matching concrete stair subtype; missing settings retain the documented straight-stair defaults, and previews use the same registry.
- `Street3D` owns its parent-local plan path, serialized typed `StreetProfilePoint` elevation samples, cross-section settings, strict `slope > 25 degrees` stair classification, generated mesh cache, and collision. `StreetGeometry3D` builds one continuously sloped road plus mirrored raised kerbs and footpaths; only the kerbs/footpaths become steps. `StreetGeometryResolver` supplies two kinds of transient junction geometry for coplanar siblings coordinated by one `Building3D`, or explicitly grouped as generated siblings by `LowPolyTerrain3D`. For mid-span crossings and T-junctions it assigns per-segment cuts: every participant removes buried road side faces and kerb/footpath runs across the other road, while the through street owns a T-junction road surface and scene order owns a crossing surface. For shared-endpoint junctions (two or more streets whose endpoints coincide, as generated mask streets produce at every branch and corner) it instead assigns per-end miter joins: the arms are sorted by heading around the junction and each adjacent pair's touching road edge, kerb, and footpath are extended onto one shared corner, so kerbs and footpaths connect across the junction instead of leaving a gap. Endpoint-to-endpoint pairs are excluded from cutting so the miter is not trimmed back. Each arm retreats its road band to the shared road corners and fills the junction centre with a triangle wedge fanning from the junction point to those corners, so the arms tile the intersection with no overlap (no coplanar z-fighting) and no hole. Miter joins are recomputed each refresh, never serialized. Authored paths and sibling nodes remain unchanged. Vertically separated crossings, near-collinear spans, and automatic-stair segments do not merge or miter. Street3D publishes the generic `is_terrain_street_source()` / `get_world_terrain_corridor()` contract and `terrain_corridor_changed` signal. Resampling replaces automatic samples while preserving manual samples at matching path stations. Compatible terrain may shape its own supporting bed from the published corridor and may instantiate transient Street3D nodes from mask-derived paths, but never owns visible street shape or materials. Persistent manual height edits require an authored Street3D; generated mask streets are deliberately replaced during terrain rebuild.
- `BuildingGenerationSpec` owns the common version/name/seed/grid envelope for deterministic batch generation. JSON is an interchange format; a missing or `building` type produces `BuildingSpec`, while `street` produces `StreetSpec`, and each concrete resource validates only its own dimensions and scope.
- `BuildingSpecCompiler` dispatches typed `BuildingGenerationSpec` resources. `BuildingSpec` retains legacy/default building generation; `StreetSpec` compiles explicit 3D path heights into one authored Street3D. Compilation failures return no partial assembly.
- `generate_building.gd` is the headless JSON-to-`.tscn` entry point. It stays an authoring tool outside the runtime app shell, prints a machine-readable report, optionally writes the report, creates output directories, and saves only successful compiler results.
- `BuildingThumbnailRenderer` owns the reusable isolated `SubViewport`, entrance-aware isometric camera, preview ground, lighting/environment, two-frame renderer warm-up, background-only image rejection, PNG output, and contact-sheet composition used by visual generation. It never changes the packed building scene.
- `generate_variants.gd` duplicates one valid base spec across consecutive seeds, compiles and saves each successful building, asks `BuildingThumbnailRenderer` for one fixed thumbnail per variant, and writes a versioned manifest mapping indices/seeds/resolved parameters to scene and image paths. Its contact sheet uses successful manifest order, and per-variant failures remain explicit rather than being silently omitted.
- Visual variant generation requires a graphical display/rendering driver. It fails before generation when Godot uses the dummy headless display; automation may use a tiny off-screen native window. Scene-only `generate_building.gd` remains fully headless.
- Generator schema/generator version 1 supports two concrete types: `building` retains one rectangular storey, one required exterior entrance, repeated facade windows, optional porch pillars, footprint-cell jitter, and one roof; `street` accepts explicit multi-point 3D path heights and the road/kerb/footpath/stair cross-section settings. Neither type infers runtime terrain or unrelated building content.
- `WallGeometryResolver` owns collinear wall merge detection, intersecting-wall detection, opening-placement conflict checks, geometry-only sibling clip assignment, and propagation of openings across collinear sibling walls. `RoofGeometryResolver` owns same-eave roof overlap calculations, visible-area tests, covered-region refresh, polygon clipping, and transient roof underside surfaces supplied to walls. `StreetGeometryResolver` owns sibling centerline intersection detection, transient mid-span/T-junction cut assignment, and shared-endpoint miter-join assignment that extends kerbs and footpaths onto shared junction corners. Resolver instances are implementation services created from one building root's current children; they are not nodes, serialized authored state, or independent building owners.
- Selecting authored `Wall3D`, `Floor3D`, `Street3D`, `Stairs3D`, `Rail3D`, `Pillar3D`, `Roof3D`, a concrete `Window3D`/`Door3D` subtype, or a prop placed by the plugin updates the active building tool mode through the dock/toolbar path; typed Window/Door inheritance determines the opening mode, and placed prop roots carry a persistent plugin marker.
- Debug wireframe is a dock-owned global display preference, not authored block state or a factory argument. `BuildingWireframe` discovers each root or multipart source `MeshInstance3D` and temporarily assigns a native spatial-shader `wireframe` pass directly to its RenderingServer instance, bypassing authored node properties and scene mutation. It must not add debug nodes to the edited scene tree, extract triangle faces, deduplicate edges on the CPU, or allocate duplicate mesh geometry. Existing authored material overlays are chained as the debug material's next pass and restored when debug display is disabled. Normal wireframes retain back-face culling and depth testing so invisible surface triangles remain hidden; X-ray mode explicitly disables only depth testing and is the opt-in path for hidden geometry. `BuildingMesh3D` provides the shared transient display API for all present and future mesh blocks, `BuildingOpening3D` applies the same API to its generated child parts, and marked prop roots use the recursive utility directly. Changing enabled state, color, or X-ray mode must not rebuild source mesh, generated parts, collision, authored properties, or scene hierarchy.
- The dock owns shared creation defaults and selected-style defaults. It shows only controls applicable to the active tool and selected concrete style, persists those values in editor project metadata, and applies them consistently to previews and committed nodes without serializing defaults on `Building3D`.
- `Wall3D` stores zero or more typed `WallSegment` resources in its exported `segments` array. Segment zero, when present, supplies the node transform; every segment stores its own parent-local endpoints, height, thickness, and color. Changing the node-level wall height, thickness, or color applies that value to every authored segment, while editing a segment resource directly can still create intentional per-segment variation. An empty array is valid and generates no mesh or collision. Hidden `start_point`, `end_point`, and `extra_segments` compatibility aliases load legacy scenes but are not inspector-facing authored state. Shared joint endpoint drags move every connected segment endpoint together, single endpoint drags can disconnect from or reconnect to a shared joint, zero-length endpoint drags can promote another segment to index zero when the old first segment is deleted, and one low-poly mesh/collision assembly rebuilds from authored segments, sibling clip spans, roof clip surfaces, and direct child openings.
- Wall meshes miter shared non-collinear endpoints by extending wall side faces to their endpoint-direction side-line intersections without adding butt-style joint caps, keep those miter points invariant when the same span is drawn in the opposite start/end direction, keep top caps tied to wall footprints with targeted overlap clipping so 3+ split joins render filled while enclosed wall loops keep their room interiors open, omit hidden underside triangles on the shared wall base plane, and clip overlapping faces in plan space so crossings, T-junctions, and corners avoid square butt seams, open miter gaps, and z-fighting caps. Down-facing header surfaces above openings remain because they can be visible. Where a visible roof intersects a wall, geometry above the roof bottom is removed while the wall volume and side faces below it are retained across eaves, slopes, and gable ridges; when the roof covers only a middle interval, the uncovered wall spans on both sides remain complete and full-height. The covered clipping boundary intentionally generates no wall upper-face triangles because the roof supplies that surface. Retained exterior wall faces keep their original horizontal outward normals and matching winding.
- The dock persists a Wall Type of `Wall` or `Room` plus a Room-only Sides value clamped to a minimum of three. Wall mode draws one grid-snapped span and retains optional eight-way direction locking. Room mode treats the grid-snapped gesture points as opposite polygon bounds and creates one `Wall3D` node with the configured number of connected authored spans, one generated mesh/collision assembly, and one undo/redo action. Four sides preserves the axis-aligned rectangular room contract; other side counts distribute vertices around the bounded ellipse.
- When separate rooms or walls contain matching collinear overlapping spans, scene order always owns the overlap: the earlier wall keeps the shared mesh/collision and the later wall clips its duplicate portion. Authored spans remain unchanged so moving either room restores the appropriate geometry.
- Door and window placement is allowed on either wall of a matching collinear shared room boundary, independently of scene order; the wall that renders the shared span cuts the opening (authored or propagated from the clipped sibling). Crossings, T-junctions, and different-thickness spans reject the opening.
- An opening node parented to any wall of a matching collinear overlap is cut from every collinear sibling that shares the span, in both scene-order directions: `WallGeometryResolver` maps the opening into each receiving wall's segment-local span. The scene-earlier wall renders the shared mesh and cuts the visible hole; because collision is generated per wall and is not sibling-clipped, each collinear wall also cuts the opening from its own collision so the duplicated overlap collision does not block the doorway. The mapping is transient rebuild data passed alongside the sibling clip segments, never edits authored spans, and never reparents the opening node; opening size and local-transform signals refresh the affected walls.
- Wall drag edits are segment-aware: Shift-clicking a wall body inserts a joint by splitting that span into two connected segments, dragging an isolated endpoint changes only the picked segment endpoint, dragging a shared joint endpoint changes every connected segment endpoint at that joint, Option/Alt-dragging a shared joint endpoint disconnects only the picked endpoint, dragging a single endpoint near another endpoint or joint snaps it there to connect, dragging a wall body translates the wall's authored segment set, zero-length endpoint drags delete the collapsed segment or wall node through undo/redo, drag commits normalize same-wall intersections, and crossings with other wall instances refresh sibling clip geometry without merging wall nodes.
- Dragging the body of one side of a four-span rectangular room resizes that room perpendicular to the selected side, moves both connected corner endpoints, leaves the opposite side fixed, preserves opening anchors, rejects a zero-width or zero-depth result, and commits through one undo/redo action. Option/Alt-dragging a rectangular room body bypasses side resizing and translates the full authored segment set instead, grid-snapped, preserving opening anchors, through one undo/redo action. Body dragging an irregular or open wall network continues to translate the full authored segment set.
- `Floor3D` supports rectangle-corner storage and general polygon-point storage without a serialized `floor_style` property. Empty `polygon_points` selects `start_point/end_point` rectangle storage; any authored polygon points select polygon storage. Rectangle and Polygon in the dock are creation modes shared by Solid and Hole: Rectangle uses the two-corner or drag gesture, while Polygon grid-snaps each click without limiting interim vertex placement and validates the completed outline only when the first vertex is clicked or Enter is pressed. Solid footprints and authored polygon-hole outlines expose the same vertex/edge/body editing model. Dragging one vertex moves only that vertex, dragging an edge moves its two adjacent vertices together, Shift-clicking an edge inserts a snapped vertex, Option/Alt-clicking a vertex removes it while preserving the three-vertex minimum, and body dragging translates the full outline. A rectangle-corner floor without holes converts to polygon-point storage on its first shape edit; undo restores its rectangle storage. New holes serialize in `floor_hole_polygons`; legacy rectangular `floor_holes` remain supported for existing scenes. Both outer storage forms derive the node transform from their minimum X/Z top corner, extend thickness downward, and rebuild one low-poly slab mesh plus generated concave collision around rectangle or polygon holes. Holes must remain fully inside the floor and may not overlap another hole. Invalid/self-intersecting polygons and zero-area outlines are rejected when creation finishes and during committed edits, and every creation, move, reshape, insertion, and removal commits through undo/redo.
- `Stairs3D` stores opposite parent-local footprint corners in `start_point/end_point`, derives its transform from the minimum X/Z lower-entry corner plus `stair_rotation_degrees`, and rebuilds one stepped low-poly mesh plus generated concave collision from total height, step count, underside thickness, rotation, and color. The broad hidden underside quad and its two triangles are omitted; the front/back boundaries and side profiles still extend to the configured underside depth. Each visible side is triangulated as one indexed two-triangle strip per tread, with shared bottom boundary vertices, so no side triangle stretches across multiple treads as a long ear-clipping needle. Generated side-wall box collision blockers sit inside the left and right footprint edge strips under the treads and are split per tread so their top edges follow the stair profile; `HumanBody3D` cannot enter the stairs from the side, but a character already on a step can leave or jump off sideways without hitting a full-height invisible rail or collision outside the stair footprint. Stairs tool creation converts the first draw point and current draw point into opposite corners of the rotated footprint. Stairs tool edits move the body, resize edges/corners on the active stairs grid in the stairs' rotated local frame, rotate around the footprint center, preserve the existing lower-entry Y, reject zero-area footprints, and commit through undo/redo. `Stairs3D` additionally stores independent `left_rail_enabled`/`right_rail_enabled` flags, a `rail_edge_margin` inset, plus standard post-and-bar rail dimensions (height, infill thickness, rail thickness, lower-rail center height, color) matching `Rail3D`'s property names (it has no post-spacing property). When enabled, a side rail is appended into the same generated stairs mesh and concave collision using the shared `StandardRailGeometry` strategy. Structural newels remain based on their selected tread or floor: `Tread` placement upgrades the first or last tread-center post, while `Floor` placement adds a newel one tread interval beyond the run. Despite its legacy property name, `middle_newel_post_count` is the total requested newel count, including enabled lower and upper terminals. Those terminals become the first and last anchors, and only the remaining count is distributed as evenly as the discrete tread centers permit between them. If a lower terminal is disabled, the first distributed newel occupies the first tread; if an upper terminal is disabled, the last occupies the final tread. Position fallback does not promote those distributed posts into terminals: every distributed newel keeps a raked top. Only explicitly enabled lower and upper newels receive flat top overrides and horizontal handrail end sections. `infill_count_between_newels` replaces the tread-derived posts inside every adjacent newel pair with exactly the requested count, including zero. With a positive `rail_lower_height`, those infills start on the top surface of the raked base rail, and each infill's bottom corners shear along the rake so the bottom edge lies exactly on that sloped surface instead of staying flat. Setting `rail_lower_height` to `0` omits the base-rail box entirely and rebases every infill onto the tread beneath its final distributed position. Infill centers are solved so every clear gap—from newel face to infill, between infills, and from infill to newel face—is equal. Posts outside the first/last newel retain their tread-derived positions. The raked handrail joins each horizontal end at the terminal newel's stair-facing edge for tread placement or at the stair endpoint for floor placement. Its outer run ends exactly at each enabled terminal newel's outer face, never projecting past the post. Newel thickness is clamped to the handrail width so its open top remains fully covered. `rail_edge_margin` insets each enabled rail's origin from the footprint's left/right edge instead of straddling it, clamped to at most 45% of the footprint width so opposing margins on a narrow stairs cannot cross (45% of the flight width for non-straight layouts).
- `Stairs3D` owns only universal stair state, lifecycle, cache, and collision, and delegates the reusable flight/landing segment mesh primitives to the internal `stair_segment_geometry_3d.gd` strategy (mirroring the roof geometry-strategy split); each node configures one strategy instance from its authored properties and concrete layouts substitute their specialized strategy subclass; it exposes no layout selector, layout-only inspector properties, all-layout configuration API, or turning-plan helper API. New authoring represents layouts with the concrete `StraightStairs3D`, `LShapedStairs3D`, `DoubleLShapedStairs3D`, `UShapedStairs3D`, `WinderStairs3D`, and `SpiralStairs3D` subclasses. Every concrete class owns its layout identity, geometry-generation entry point, complete segment/rail plan, and layout-specific mirroring. `StraightStairs3D` owns the full single-run mesh, rail-layout, and side-blocker generation path rather than delegating concrete geometry back into the base. The internal `TurningStairs3D` intermediate owns `turn_direction` (`Left`/`Right`), `flight_width`, their focused `configure_turning_layout()` API, shared plan allocation and mirroring, raked transition-rail planning, and turning-plan collision orchestration because every non-straight layout uses them. `WinderStairs3D` alone owns the `WinderTurn` enum, `winder_turn` (90/180 degrees), fanned-segment plan construction, mirroring, collision orchestration, and `configure_winder_layout()`, with its radial-fan mesh primitives in the internal `winder_fan_geometry_3d.gd` strategy. `SpiralStairs3D` alone owns `spiral_turn_degrees` (45–1080 degrees), plan construction, mirroring, collision orchestration, and `configure_spiral_layout()`, with its radial-wedge and helical-rail mesh primitives in the internal `spiral_geometry_3d.gd` strategy. `BuildingFactory.configure_stair_layout()` is the central caller-side adapter that selects the focused concrete configuration API; `Stairs3D` itself does not know those arguments. Straight stairs expose none of those controls. Non-straight layouts subdivide the same authored footprint rectangle into an ordered segment plan — straight flights, flat landing platforms, fanned winder turns, or one radial spiral assembly — authored in per-segment rotated right-handed local frames and mirrored across the footprint's local X axis for left turns. Footprint storage, transform derivation, rotation, drawing, and body/edge/corner editing are unchanged and always operate on the bounding rectangle. `flight_width` is clamped at build time so every flight and landing fits the rectangle with positive run lengths; for Spiral it is the radial tread depth and is clamped to preserve a positive central-column radius. Winder turns always fan exactly three treads per 90 degrees of turn; the remaining requested step count is distributed across flights proportional to run length with at least one step per flight, growing the effective rising-step total when the request is too small, and every rising step shares one uniform rise so the final tread reaches the configured total height (`get_step_rise()` reflects that effective total). Landings are flat solid platforms at their entry height. Winder treads fan from a pivot on the turn's inner corner (90 degrees) or the middle of its inner edge (180 degrees) out to the outer perimeter, with a riser on each radial tread boundary and walls closing the outer perimeter and any free inner (well) edge. Spiral creates wedge tread slabs around an eight-sided solid central column, adds steps when necessary to keep each wedge at or below 45 degrees, and divides the configured height uniformly across the effective step count.
- `Stairs3D` serializes `tread_style` (`Closed`, `Open`, `Nosing`) and `nosing_depth`. `Closed` is the default and preserves the solid stepped mass for every layout. `Open` replaces flight masses with one floating slab per tread (no risers, no solid underside, slab thickness `max(stair_thickness, 0.05)` matching the spiral tread rule), turns landings into floating platform slabs with exposed undersides, and turns winder fans into floating wedge slabs with per-tread radial faces while omitting the closed layout's extra winder walls; open spiral wedges stay one slab thick. `Nosing` keeps the closed mass and appends one additive lip box overhanging each flight riser plane by `nosing_depth`, clamped at build time to 45% of the tread depth with a lip thickness strictly shallower than one rise, so the underlying stepped geometry and side profiles are unchanged; winder fans have no nosing variant and render as `Closed`. Spiral supports only Closed/Open, narrows the inherited inspector enum accordingly, and hides `nosing_depth`. Both properties still participate in the shared generated-mesh cache signature. Rails, newels, and infills are independent of tread style.
- Non-straight stair rails follow the full travel path per side: raked flight rails reuse the straight post/newel layout per flight, landings receive level rail runs along their free edges, and winder turns receive raked rail runs whose total rise is split across their legs by length. Spiral uses one chorded raked outer rail following its tread boundaries; its central column closes the inner edge, and left/right turn direction determines which side flag addresses the outer rail. Every inflection point of a flight/landing/winder path carries exactly one shared newel and the rail stays continuous across it. At each flight/transition junction the flight owns a forced junction newel that behaves like a distributed newel — on the mid of the flight's first/last tread, raked top, never terminating the handrail — while the flight's handrail, base-rail, and panel bars are cut flush at the flight boundary (a run-extent cut, not a terminal treatment) and the transition leg's bars, carrying no boundary post of their own, continue from that plane at the identical height. Interior corners between two transition legs carry one corner newel owned by the leg that starts there, and both adjacent legs extend their bars past the corner point by exactly half the handrail bar thickness so the outer faces close flush around every corner. Junction and corner newels are structural extras: they use the newel thickness but do not count toward `middle_newel_post_count`, whose requested total is shared across flights proportional to their steps. Explicit lower/upper terminal newels and horizontal handrail end sections apply only to the overall first and last flight ends; distributed and junction newels always sit on tread centers, while corner newels stand at platform corners. Transition legs follow the same vertical-rail rule as flights: each leg is one newel span, the Vertical style places exactly `infill_count_between_newels` infills per span with equal clear gaps between post faces (legs too short for the requested infills carry only their newels), and the Horizontal/Glass styles place no vertical infills there. With a base rail those infills mount on its top with bottom corners sheared along the leg's rake; without one they rebase onto the surface beneath (winder tread top, landing floor, or the leg's base diagonal). Shared newels always stand on the structural surface, never on the base rail. Transition legs own their complete post layout and suppress `StandardRailGeometry.append_rail()`'s default spacing distribution via its `distribute_default_posts` flag, so a leg whose only posts are shared with adjacent runs — for example under the Horizontal or Glass styles, a zero infill count, or a short connector — stays post-free instead of seeding duplicate posts at the transition boundaries.
- Spiral outer rails build the same canonical scalar post layout as a straight stair over the exact outer arc length. `middle_newel_post_count` therefore remains the total requested newel count; distributed newels stay on spiral tread centers; `Tread` terminals use the first/last radial tread center; `Floor` terminals continue the terminal-tread post cadence by one full interval tangent to the lower/upper endpoint; every adjacent newel pair receives the exact requested infill count; base-rail and no-base post heights follow the radial tread sequence; and explicit terminal posts retain the shared flat-top and horizontal-handrail behavior. Posts and newels continue to use `StandardRailGeometry`, while its continuous-member emission is disabled for Spiral. The handrail and each active lower rail, horizontal infill, or glass panel are instead emitted as one helical indexed sweep sampled at no more than 7.5 degrees per section, with shared strip vertices and smoothly rotating side normals. Spiral rail generation creates no per-tread chord runs, joint prisms, overlap collars, or joint patches.
- Non-straight stair collision keeps the per-tread side-wall box blockers in each flight's rotated frame and adds one solid box per landing plus thin per-tread boxes along winder free edges or the spiral outer chord, all tagged with the same `stairs_side_wall_collision` metadata; the concave walking-surface collision continues to follow the full generated mesh, including the spiral column. Open tread styles keep the same side blockers while the landing box shrinks to the floating slab and spiral edge boxes match each wedge's actual vertical extent.
- Stair handrail termination is exclusive to explicitly enabled lower and upper newels. Distributed newels, including first/final-tread fallbacks and the forced tread-mid junction newels at non-straight layout transitions, may widen their welded underside opening but never set horizontal end transitions; the raked handrail remains continuous across every such post. At layout transitions the flight run's bars end flush at the flight boundary (a run-extent cut, not a terminal treatment) so the transition leg's bars continue them without a height step.
- Both rail callers expose three shared infill styles through one `infill_style` property. `Vertical Rail` uses the configured exact infill count in each newel span and the optional base rail. `Horizontal Rail` removes vertical infills and adds the same configured count of continuous horizontal infill rails, each with the square `infill_rail_thickness` cross-section, with equal clear gaps above the base rail, between the infill rails, and below the handrail. Without a base rail, the same equal-gap layout starts at the rail base. Infill geometry is clamped to the handrail cross-section the same way newels are, so no infill exceeds the rail's width regardless of the configured `infill_rail_thickness`. `Glass Panel` removes rail infills entirely and adds one thin continuous translucent panel (vertex-tinted with `StandardRailGeometry.GLASS_PANEL_COLOR`) spanning from the base-rail top (or rail base without one) to the handrail underside; both callers switch their generated material to depth-pre-pass alpha transparency (`TRANSPARENCY_ALPHA_DEPTH_PRE_PASS`) while that style is active, so the opaque posts, handrail, and steps in the same surface keep correct depth sorting while the panel blends. Stair versions shear horizontal infill and glass panels along the same rake as their handrail; standalone versions remain level.
- `Rail3D` stores one parent-local `start_point/end_point` span plus infill style, total newel count, per-span infill count, newel thickness, height, infill rail thickness (`infill_rail_thickness`), rail thickness, lower-rail center height, color, rebuild state, and collision behavior — the same ordering `Stairs3D`'s rail group and both dock sections use. Its transform follows the horizontal span. Newels are distributed evenly from the start endpoint through the end endpoint; every adjacent pair receives the configured number of infills with equal clear gaps between post faces. The handrail and base-rail run is pinned to the endpoint newels' outer faces through explicit run overrides, so changing the infill rail thickness never changes the rail length. Newels remain based on the authored ground plane. When the lower rail is enabled, infills begin on its top face; setting its height to zero removes the lower-rail box and rebases infills onto the ground plane. Rail tool creation and edits snap to the active Rail grid, preserve the base Y, support body translation and endpoint resizing, reject undersized spans, and commit through undo/redo. `Rail3D` and `Stairs3D` both delegate span population to `StandardRailGeometry.apply_infill_count_between_newels()` and `redistribute_infills_between_newels()`, and both use its shared lower-rail presence/top-height calculations. They then pass the resulting parallel position/base/thickness/top arrays to `append_rail()`, which embeds the canonical rail into the caller's axes and applies a linear height shear for stairs. Thus level and stair rails share newel sizing, exact infill counts, face-gap spacing, base-rail mounting/removal, welded handrail openings, omitted post caps, and collision-ready geometry; only the caller-specific newel anchors and no-base fallback surface differ.
- `Pillar3D` stores only universal pillar state: parent-local `base_point`, lower radius, optional upper-radius override, height, upper/lower rim settings, color, rebuild state, and collision behavior. `RoundPillar3D` and `TaperedPillar3D` inherit the internal variable-sided layer that owns `side_count`; `SquarePillar3D` and `OctagonalPillar3D` own their fixed geometry without exposing that unused control. Pillar tool edits move the body or resize radius on the active pillar grid, preserve the existing base Y, reject undersized radii, and commit through undo/redo.
- `Roof3D` stores universal roof state: opposite parent-local rectangle corners, thickness, overhang, rotation, color, covered render regions, rebuild state, and collision behavior. It declares no concrete style keys, performs no style-key dispatch, and exposes no polygon-footprint, face-angle, or hip-gable public methods. `FlatRoof3D`, `ShedRoof3D`, `GableRoof3D`, `HipRoof3D`, and `DomeRoof3D` identify the serialized style and select their matching geometry strategy directly; shared cross-style clipping queries go through `RoofStyleGeometryFactory`, which rejects unknown styles instead of falling back to Flat. Only `FlatRoof3D` additionally owns optional parent-local `polygon_points` and its polygon editing/query API. Empty Flat points use rectangle-corner storage, while non-empty points use polygon storage. Rectangle and Polygon in the Flat-roof dock controls differ only during creation; Polygon creation grid-snaps each click without limiting interim vertex placement and validates the completed outline only when the first vertex is clicked or Enter is pressed. Both expose the same outline editing: drag a vertex, drag an edge and its two endpoints, Shift-click an edge to insert a snapped vertex, Option/Alt-click a vertex to remove it, or drag the body to translate the footprint. The first outline reshape of a rectangular Flat roof promotes its four corners to polygon storage, and undo restores the rectangle representation. Shed, gable, hip, and dome roofs remain rectangular. The internal sloped-roof layer owns the face-angle property and accessors used by those styles; only `HipRoof3D` owns the hip gable drop and the `hip_shape` substyle (Standard, Pyramid, Hexagonal, Octagon) plus their accessors. `HipRoof3D` selects its geometry strategy from the shape: Standard uses the ridge geometry, while Pyramid, Hexagonal, and Octagon share one pavilion geometry strategy varying only by base-side count. The hip style stays a single serialized style; `RoofStyleGeometryFactory` expands the authored `hip_shape` parameter into the internal pavilion geometry for cross-style clipping queries. Each style delegates its generated height, surface sampling, top faces, topology, and edge splits to its matching geometry strategy through an extensible parameter dictionary; the generic strategy signature contains no Hip-only argument. Floor slabs and unclipped Flat polygon roofs share `PolygonPrismGeometry` for winding normalization, triangulation, top/underside faces, direct boundary extrusion, and collision faces. Flat overhang computes one bounded miter point per authored point instead of accepting a clipping-library bevel or split contour, so acute footprints retain one render corner per authored corner. Covered regions may still introduce transient mesh intersection points without changing authored polygon storage. Dome roofs use a deterministic elliptical cap made from 16 radial facets and five vertical bands; the rectangular footprint and overhang define its ellipse, the configured angle defines its peak rise from the shorter radius, and a 45-degree square dome is hemispherical. Simple hip roofs use a horizontal ridge along the longer footprint axis, two triangular short faces, two trapezoid long faces, and the same configured angle on all faces; square hip footprints degenerate to a centered apex. Positive hip gable drop clips the standard hip ridge ends downward from the peak and extends the ridge as needed to preserve the configured face angle, producing a half-hip or jerkinhead variant; the pavilion hip shapes ignore it. Pavilion hip shapes instead raise a closed base ring to a single centered apex whose rise comes from the shorter footprint radius and the configured angle: the Pyramid shape uses the four footprint corners so it covers the whole rectangle, while the Hexagonal and Octagon shapes inscribe a regular six- or eight-sided polygon in the footprint bounds. Roof tool edits preserve the existing eave-plane Y, reject invalid or zero-area footprints, recompute sibling roof covers and wall clipping, and commit through undo/redo. Rectangular and pitched roofs retain rotated edge/corner resize and center rotation behavior; polygon Flat roofs derive orientation from their authored vertices.
- `BuildingMesh3D` owns native-gizmo transform reconciliation for every block. Because each block derives its node transform from authored parent-local properties, a native Move/Rotate/Scale edit only changes the raw transform; the base measures that as a delta (`native_transform_delta` = current transform times the authored transform inverse) and blocks bake it back through `_bake_native_delta`. Concrete blocks override `_authored_transform` (the value `_sync_transform_from_points()`/`_sync_transform_from_base()` assigns), `_bake_native_delta`, `capture_native_transform_state`, and `restore_native_transform_state`, and report `supports_native_transform() == true`. Baking maps translation to the anchor/points, yaw to the block's rotation field (rectangle floors promote to polygon storage when rotated), and per-axis scale to authored sizes (footprint, run, length, radius, height) before the node scale returns to 1. Pitch and roll are not authored building-block states, so native deltas are flattened to translation, yaw, and scale before baking, and unsupported tilt is reset. Blocks that store a scalar size plus a rotation field (roof, stairs) read scale and yaw from their own local frame (`native_local_scale`/`native_local_yaw_degrees`) rather than the parent-space delta, so a rotated block scales along its rotated axes; point-based blocks transform authored points by the delta directly, which is already rotation-correct. Grid snapping applies to placement only: the block's anchor/reference point snaps to the grid and the whole block shifts by that single offset, so the scaled size and geometry are preserved exactly instead of being quantized. The plugin drives detection in Select mode: it snapshots authored state on left-press for every building block in each selected node's subtree. For direct block selections, Move and yaw Rotate bake only the selected block's placement even when it owns descendant blocks, so children keep their local positions and authored parent-local points. For non-placement parent edits, after the gizmo finalizes it converts the edited node's world-space transform delta into each affected block's parent frame, applies it through `bake_external_delta`, refreshes sibling roof/wall clipping, and records `EditorUndoRedoManager` actions using the capture/restore hooks. Because a block's own node transform is unchanged when an ancestor is scaled, the bake reads scale/yaw from the effective transform (delta times authored) rather than the block's raw transform. Moving a non-block container (for example the `Building3D` root) leaves the translation on that container transform so children keep their local positions and authored parent-local points. Scaling or rotating a non-block container snaps the selected container once, bakes that shared adjusted delta into all descendant blocks so child spacing is preserved, and resets the container to its pre-edit transform. Openings do not participate; they stay wall-anchored.
- `BuildingOpening3D` is the shared persistent child-node base for window and door holes. Its `opening_width`, `opening_height`, segment-local position, frame properties, and collision settings define the common opening rectangle and casing contract consumed by the wall mesh compiler. Openings are mapped to the nearest wall segment and restored into that segment's updated frame when wall geometry changes (including endpoint, joint, extra-segment, and whole-wall drags).
- `Window3D` owns only behavior applicable to every window opening and generates no style geometry. The internal `window_pane_3d.gd` layer owns pane depth/color plus plain-pane primitives for glazed pane-bearing styles. Every concrete class owns its geometry entry point: single/double windows choose their pane layout, `GridWindow3D` builds its muntins, `LouveredWindow3D` builds its slats, `TransomWindow3D` builds its rail, `ArchedWindow3D` builds its stepped fillers, and `WindowFrame3D` intentionally builds no pane content.
- `Door3D` owns only behavior and defaults applicable to every door opening and generates no style geometry. The internal `leafed_door_3d.gd` layer owns panel depth/color plus plain-leaf primitives for styles with physical leaves. Every concrete class owns its geometry entry point: single/double doors choose their leaf layout, `GlazedDoor3D` builds its solid/glass split, `GlazedGridDoor3D` adds its lite muntins, `PanelDoor3D` builds its inset grid, `DutchDoor3D` builds its split leaves and mid rail, and frame styles intentionally build no leaf content.
- The editor must instantiate the concrete class for the selected style for both previews and committed nodes; style properties and style geometry remain in that class, not in conditionals on `BuildingOpening3D`, `Window3D`, or `Door3D`. Primary parts (panes, panels/leaves, slats) carry collision while decorative overlays do not, and style details never change the opening rectangle the wall compiler reads. `frame_sides`, `frame_protrusion`, and `wall_thickness` remain shared base properties and only change the casing depth.
- New overlapping collinear walls of matching thickness and height merge by extending the retained wall endpoints instead of creating duplicate spans.
- Non-collinear intersecting walls sharing a base plane remain separate authored `Wall3D` nodes: sibling spans are supplied as non-rendered clip segments, each wall renders only its own authored spans, buried side and top-cap geometry is removed in plan space, same-height top-cap ownership follows scene order, roof underside cuts are supplied as transient non-rendered clip surfaces, and undo/redo refreshes clip geometry without reparenting children or removing other wall nodes. Heights from sibling walls may split a wall's generated cells only when their plan footprints overlap; unrelated walls must not introduce mismatched roof-clip contours.
- `WallSegment`, `Wall3D`, `Roof3D`, and `BuildingOpening3D` emit authored-geometry signals for clipping-relevant changes. `Building3D` dynamically connects direct wall/roof children, coalesces those notifications into one deferred update, and asks the transient resolvers to refresh clipping data without per-frame signature polling. Resolver-derived clip assignments and covered regions stay silent so they cannot create refresh feedback loops.
- Serialized procedural geometry is a validated cache, not authored state. `BuildingMesh3D` supplies hidden source/clipping signatures and cache helpers shared by `Wall3D`, `Floor3D`, `Stairs3D`, `Rail3D`, `Pillar3D`, and `Roof3D`; scene loading reuses a matching root mesh and recreates generated collision/debug children independently. Generated mesh and material resources plus authored `WallSegment` resources are scene-local, so editing one packed `Building3D` instance cannot alter another instance's geometry or cache. Wall sibling/roof clips and roof covered regions participate in clipping signatures, so unchanged coordinator refreshes do not rebuild meshes while changed clipping conditions do. `BuildingOpening3D` stores an equivalent signature plus compact generated part-mesh descriptors because its window/door styles consist of multiple child meshes rather than one root mesh; load recreates child nodes and collision from those cached resources without rerunning style geometry. Legacy scenes without cache metadata rebuild once.
- Editor commands that create, move, resize, restore, undo, or redo walls and roofs refresh the complete building clipping chain synchronously: roof covered regions resolve first, then wall sibling and roof-underside clips. Partial wall-only or roof-only command refreshes must not rely on a later signal turn to repair cross-family clipping.
- Editor integration is event-driven and controller-based. `plugin.gd` keeps only plugin lifecycle, dock wiring, editor-selection sync, and tool-mode dispatch; each building tool's dock defaults, drawing previews, hover highlighting, picking, drag editing, and tool-specific keys live in an internal per-tool controller under `editor/` (one shared placement controller covers window, door, and prop). Controllers are plugin-lifetime objects that reach editor services only through the shared `editor/building_tool_context.gd`, undo/redo actions bind only plugin-lifetime objects or edited nodes, and controllers never call each other — cross-tool needs (wall raycasts, opening validation, the shared wall grid step) go through the context. The viewport toolbar, its generated icons, and native viewport button mutual exclusivity live in `editor/native_toolbar_integration.gd`. Tool-mode changes push active state into viewport overlays, overlay parent-order notifications keep active overlays in front, and native viewport button signals maintain mutual exclusivity with building tools. `Wall3D` uses restartable one-shot editor rebuild requests to update visuals immediately and defer collision rebuilding without an idle `_process()` loop.
- Generated wall solid collision cells, exact concave collision from roof-clipped retained wall faces, floor/stairs/rail/pillar/roof collision, floor-hole side geometry, and opening frame parts are rebuild artifacts identified by metadata; they are not author-facing source nodes.

- Concrete Winder and Spiral stair classes own their layout-specific mesh, rail, and collision algorithms. `Stairs3D` retains only universal stair lifecycle plus shared flight, landing, segment, and rail assembly primitives.
- Spiral stairs accept only Closed/Open tread state: unsupported assignments normalize to Closed, the inspector hides Nosing and nosing depth, and the dock disables Nosing.
- The mesh infrastructure base is internal. Concrete editor registration metadata is derived from the canonical factory script registries, and unknown opening styles are rejected instead of crossing the Window/Door boundary through a fallback.

## Building Block Style Pattern

Use this pattern for every future serialized building-block family that has distinct styles:

1. The family base class owns only properties and behavior applicable to every style. It may provide low-level mesh, material, collision, lifecycle, and transform primitives, but it must not export a style selector or branch on style to generate geometry.
2. Add an internal intermediate class only when a real subset shares authored properties or primitives. Examples are pane-bearing windows, leaf-bearing doors, variable-sided pillars, and sloped roofs. Styles outside that subset must not inherit unused inspector properties.
3. Every serialized style has a concrete class. That class owns style identity, style-only exported properties, defaults, and its geometry entry point. Large reusable shape math may live in a one-to-one style geometry strategy, but it must remain selected by the concrete class.
4. Editor factories map persisted dock selections—style keys or concrete script resources—to concrete scripts. Both previews and committed nodes come from the same factory; changing a preview style recreates the preview with the correct class instead of mutating a style flag.
5. Retired style keys and base-class style properties are removed rather than retained through compatibility adapters. Style routing belongs only in the current factory/resolver boundary.
6. Frame-only or empty styles intentionally override the geometry entry point with no inner content. Shared dimensions consumed by another compiler—such as an opening rectangle read by `Wall3D`—remain on the family base and cannot be changed by decorative style geometry.
7. Tests must verify inheritance, inspector-property ownership, factory class selection, each style's generated geometry, and rejection of unsupported styles. A base-class property regression is a contract failure even when the generated mesh still looks correct.
8. A tool option that performs an operation is not automatically a serialized style. `Room` creates a four-segment `Wall3D`, and `Floor Hole` modifies a `Floor3D`; neither needs a `Room3D` or `FloorHole3D` subtype unless it becomes persistent polymorphic scene state.
9. Abstract family bases and internal intermediate layers are path-extended implementation details, not globally named or editor-creatable custom types. `BuildingFactory` owns the canonical concrete script registries, and editor custom-type registration is derived from those registries instead of maintaining duplicate lists.

Before adding a style switch to an existing block, first decide whether the distinction persists in the scene. Persistent polymorphism follows this hierarchy; one-shot editor operations remain coordinator/plugin workflows.

## Governance

- Keep building-editor scene mutations undoable through `EditorUndoRedoManager`; placed wall, room, floor, stairs, rail, pillar, roof, prop, and opening mutations — including deletion of a zero-length wall segment or its final wall node — must go through it so undo/redo restores scene hierarchy and rebuilt meshes together.
- If wall segment storage, floor style/vertex/corner/hole storage, stairs footprint storage, rail span/component storage, pillar base/radius storage, roof footprint storage, segment splitting, child reparenting, opening semantics, generated collision, merge behavior, or editor interaction changes, update this contract and [`feature.md`](feature.md).
- If the building-spec or variant-manifest schema, deterministic resolution order, supported generator scope, report fields, preview camera/lighting contract, or save-on-success behavior changes, increment the appropriate schema/generator version and update this contract, [`feature.md`](feature.md), the example spec, and generator regression coverage together.
- Do not treat `Building3D` buildings as runtime story targets or save/resume anchors without defining and documenting a narrow consumer-project contract.
